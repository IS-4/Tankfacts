Experimental Tanks

Experimental tanks, also known as prototype tanks, are vehicles that are designed and built for testing new technologies, concepts, or features that may eventually find their way into production tanks. These tanks are typically created by military organizations or defense contractors to explore and evaluate innovative ideas in armored warfare. Experimental tanks can vary greatly in design and purpose, but they generally serve the following purposes: 1. Technology Testing: Experimental tanks are used to assess and validate new technologies, such as advanced armor materials, propulsion systems, weapon systems, targeting systems, or other cutting-edge features. These tanks allow engineers and military personnel to experiment with these technologies in a controlled environment before considering them for operational use. 2. Concept Validation: Experimental tanks may be used to explore new tactical or operational concepts. For example, they might test alternative crew configurations, variations in armor layout, unconventional weapon placements, or different mobility solutions. These tanks help assess the viability and effectiveness of these conceptual designs. 3. Performance Evaluation: Experimental tanks are utilized to evaluate the performance of specific components or systems under real-world conditions. This might involve testing the reliability and durability of engines, suspension systems, or other crucial components. By gathering data on these parameters, designers can identify areas for improvement or optimization. 4. Future Development: The insights gained from experimental tanks can inform the development of future production tanks. Lessons learned, successful technologies, or effective concepts identified during testing can be integrated into upcoming tank designs, enhancing their capabilities and ensuring they adhere to the evolving demands of modern warfare. It's important to note that not all experimental tanks progress beyond the prototype stage. Many experimental tanks remain as concept platforms or serve solely for research purposes. However, some prototypes eventually evolve into operational tanks if their performance and potential benefits are deemed significant enough to warrant further investment and development.

Object. 279

The Object 279 is a Soviet experimental heavy tank developed during the Cold War era. It was designed and produced by the Soviet Union in the late 1950s and early 1960s. The main objective behind the development of the Object 279 was to create a tank that could withstand various types of threats on the battlefield, including nuclear explosions. The Object 279 featured some unique design elements that set it apart from conventional tanks of that era. Its most distinctive feature was its four-track suspension system, which provided increased mobility and ensured better weight distribution over different types of terrain. This configuration also helped enhance the tank's stability and overcome obstacles. Additionally, the Object 279 had exceptionally thick armored plates ranging from 305mm to 375mm, providing superior protection against conventional projectiles and even nuclear blasts. Its turret was heavily armored as well, and it housed a 130mm smoothbore gun as the primary armament. Despite its advanced design and promising features, the Object 279 never went into mass production or saw active service. Only four prototypes were built due to various reasons, including the high complexity of the tank, difficulties associated with its maintenance and repair, and advancements in missile technology that rendered its design less relevant. Today, the surviving prototypes of the Object 279 are displayed in military museums, serving as a testament to the Soviet Union's innovative approach to tank design during the Cold War.
